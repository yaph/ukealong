  • Caspar Babypants - Always Keep a Ukulele in Your Trunk

    December 27, 2018

    For educational use. Optimized for GCEA ukulele. Key of A. As performed by Caspar Babypants (Chris Ballew), used with permission! (Thanks, Caspar Babypants!) The image for the video comes from the Kala Brand Instruments website (Kala Ukuleles). On babypantsmusic.com, Caspar Babypants describes the inspiration for this song: "I was watching a documentary about George Harrison and in that documentary Tom Petty told a story about George coming to his house to give him a ukulele. George and Tom strolled through the garden playing ukuleles having a grand old time. When George went to leave he asked Tom if he wanted more ukuleles. Tom said he was okay with one ukulele but George insisted and opened the trunk of his car to reveal a HUGE pile of ukuleles. The moment Tom Petty finished telling the story I realized I had to write a song about this incredible moment. I think it's good advice to always keep a ukulele within arms reach! You never know when you need to elevate your mood with the sparkly sound of a uke." I really like this song for a number of reasons. First, it pays tribute to George Harrison, one of the Beatles. The song also pays tribute in its actual sound, which is reminiscent (to me) to "Yellow Submarine." Second, the song is about ukuleles. Finally, the song offers some opportunities to stretch your playing ability with some more difficult chords (C#min, F#7) and some fingerpicking.   • Burl Ives - Aunt Rhody

    June 14, 2018

    For educational use. Optimized for GCEA ukulele. Key of F and F minor. As performed by Burl Ives. Have you ever stopped to wonder why so many of our childhood songs are so dark? Remember...”Ring Around The Rosy” is supposedly about the Black Plague. I have been looking for more songs to do, and I want to make sure that we are providing content for beginner players, too. The directory has a lot of one, two, three, and four chord songs. But I know there are songs that are missing, so I looked in the table of contents for a ukulele method book, and this song is one of the early songs in that book. Getting past the depressing lyrics (If Aunt Rhody only cared about the goose for a feather bed, she can still do that, can’t she?), the song is strong from a musical perspective—with two chords in a verse, and switching to minor. You do need to be able to play f minor—but this song would be an effective way to learn. It is also a little haunting to hear Burl Ives sing in a minor key—creepy.
